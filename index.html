This little render toy can change camera projection methods so you can explore each one.

One Point Perspective (Most Common)

Most video games today use the very fast projection method One Point Perspective. Imagine a 3D object and trace a line from each point on the object to the camera. place a canvas in front of the camera and draw a point everywhere the line intersects the canvas.

Pros Cons
Computationally efficient Barrel Distortion where things get smaller 
Keeps lines straight Objects become larger and stretched at the edges of the screen as FOV approaches 180 degrees

 

Curvilinear: 

This projection method places a dot based on the angle that the point is off-center. If the line to a point is 25 degrees up from the camera then it is drawn 25 pixels above the center of the canvas. This is very slow mathematically so another way to think about it is instead of objects getting smaller as their z distance increases they get smaller as their true euclidean distance from the camera increases. These two ways to understand curvilinear may not seem equivalent but they form the same picture. Because this projection is based on angles instead of a flat plane we can render more than a 180-degree FOV.

Pros Cons
Full 360-degree FOV Barrel Distortion, A flat plane looks like it's closer in the middle (because it actually is!)
Better preserves the size of objects. All straight lines become curved 

(Central) Cylindrical Projection

Cylindrical projection combines both of these methods to provide a good field of view in the horizontal X-axis where players want it but not requiring as much computation as curvilinear projection. It is commonly used in world maps because it is easy to understand. Imagine a cylinder around the camera through which you trace lines from the object to the camera through. Again simply draw a point where the line intersects the cylinder, then unwrap the cylinder. Vertically it can only approach 180 degrees but horizontally it can achieve a 360-degree FOV with little distortion.

Orthographic Projection

Orthographic projection is often used in engineering because it is the simplest to calculate. It simply ignores distance. It starts with a flat canvas in front of the camera. Now launch the canvas through the sceen straight from the camera. Every point it intersects is draw there on the canvas
